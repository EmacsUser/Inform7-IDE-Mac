4.9. Describing actions

Thus far we have applied rules only to specific actions, such as "taking the napkin". But it is in describing whole categories of actions that Inform really capitalises on the concise, expressive power of natural language, and this will enable us to design much more sophisticated rules with only a handful of extra words typed.

Firstly, the special description "doing something" matches any action, so that "doing something to the napkin" (or "... with the napkin", which means the same) would match "taking the napkin", "eating the napkin" and many more. Thus the following would put its object entirely out of bounds:

Instead of doing something to the cucumber sandwich, say "Lady Bracknell stares disapprovingly down her pince-nez at you, in a way which no amount of hunger or curiosity could overcome."

Secondly, any object mentioned can be described in a wide range of ways. Instead of writing "the cucumber sandwich", we could say "something edible", or even just "something". Thus:

Instead of throwing something at a closed openable door, say "Or you could just use the handle like anyone else, of course."

Thirdly, we can add a requirement about where the action in question is taking place.

Instead of taking something in the Supernatural Void, say "In this peculiar mist you feel unable to grasp anything."

Like the objects to which the action applies, the location can itself be vaguely defined:

Instead of listening in a dead end, say "You strain to hear further clues as to the course of the underground river, but to no avail."

It may be worth recalling here that when we create a new kind of value, we also create new adjectives which can be used to describe things:

Colour is a kind of value. The colours are red, blue and green. A building block is a kind of thing. A building block has a colour. Instead of taking a green building block, say "For some reason you have an aversion to the colour green."
