18.5. The meaning of "is"

Another small discrepancy between assertions and conditions turns up if we ask a surprisingly difficult question: what does "is" mean? There was much amusement when this became an issue in the absurd Senate trial of President Clinton, but the definition of "to be" spans a heroic twelve columns in the Oxford English Dictionary. Among the many meanings offered are:

OED 5a. to have or occupy a place somewhere
OED 9b. to have a place among the things distinguished by a specified quality
OED 10. to exist as the thing known by a certain name; to be identical with

All three of these are used by Inform:

OED 5a. The cargo trunk is in the attic.
OED 9b. The cargo trunk is an openable container.
OED 10. if the noun is the cargo trunk ...

OED meanings 5a and 9b are not really very different as far as Inform is concerned: they both say that something belongs to some larger class of things. "The cargo trunk is in the attic" means that the trunk is one among the things in the attic; "the cargo trunk is an openable container" means that it is among the openable containers. (In computer programming terms this is a sort of matching operator, like "=~" in the Perl scripting language.) On the other hand, OED meaning 10 is very different. If somebody reads out a line of code from the C programming language,

if (x == 7) ...

they will probably say the words "If x is 7": here, the "is" means "equals".

So "X is Y" has two essentially different meanings to Inform: "X matches Y" and "X equals Y". Both can be used in conditions, but only the first can be used in assertions. That is why Inform will reject the assertion

The cargo trunk is the oak tree.

(which must be a lie) even though it will allow exactly the same sentence structure in a condition:

if the noun is the oak tree...

This all sounds like a pedantic digression, and perhaps it is. (An OED 9b "is", by the way.) But it has a moral: that "is" can do far more than the simple value comparisons offered by most computer programming languages.
