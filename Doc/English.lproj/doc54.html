5.4. On "is"

What does "is" mean? There was much amusement when this became an issue in the absurd Senate trial of President Clinton, but the definition of "to be" spans a heroic twelve columns in the Oxford English Dictionary.

Some descriptions are exact, in referring unambiguously to a specific thing whatever the circumstances: but most are vague. If we go back to:

The cargo trunk is an openable container.

This sentence tells us that an exact description (the cargo trunk) "is" a vague one (an openable container). Here "is" means "has a place among the things distinguished by a specified quality" (OED meaning 9b: Inform also allows 9a and 9c but not 9d, for any OED junkies reading this). Inform also makes use of the more complicated meaning "to have or occupy a place somewhere" (OED 5a), as in

The cargo trunk is in the attic.

On the other hand, Inform will reject

The cargo trunk is the oak tree.

And this is not inconsistent, because although it looks the same, here we have a different meaning of the verb "to be": "to exist as the thing known by a certain name; to be identical with" (OED 10).

We might call these meanings "matches" and "equals" for short, and they behave very differently when we come to conditions. Consider:

... if the noun is the cargo trunk ... ("equals")
... if the cargo trunk is an openable container ... ("matches")

Most computer programming languages would strictly distinguish them: Perl, for instance, would write "=~" and "==" respectively. But Inform follows English and writes "is" for both meanings, and for the OED 5a meaning too, as we saw in only the previous section:

Definition: a room is occupied if a person is in it.

The condition buried in here is "a person is in it". Here "is" stands for a deceptively complicated question, involving not only the specific thing (the room) but also everything else in the world (the possible occupiers), and putting constraints on both things involved - one having to be a room, the other a person. Not bad for just five words.

This all sounds like a pedantic digression, and perhaps it is. (A 9b "is", by the way.) But it has a moral: that "is" can do far more than the simple value comparisons offered by most computer programming languages.
