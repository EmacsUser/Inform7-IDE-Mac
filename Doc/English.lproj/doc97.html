7.14. Phrases to decide

We can create new conditions by defining a phrase with "to decide whether" (or equivalently "to decide if"):

To decide whether a dangerous place:
if in darkness then yes;
if the Control Room has been visited then no;
yes.

As can be seen, such a phrase must always end with "yes" or "no". We can now write, for instance,

if a dangerous place then ...

Similarly, to decide something else:

To decide which treasure is the grand prize:
if the Dark Room has been visited then result the silver bars;
result the plover's egg.

(Supposing, that is, that a kind called "treasure" has been created and that the plover's egg and the silver bars both belong to it.) And we can now say

if taking the grand prize then ...

As this is something of a dialect difference between English speakers, "what" and "which" are synonymous here, i.e., we could equally well write:

To decide what treasure is the grand prize: ...
